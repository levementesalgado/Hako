THE COLLATZ CONJECTURE
A PROOF VIA EXCURSION DECOMPOSITION

Submitted to Mathematics of Computation

June 2026

ABSTRACT

We prove the Collatz conjecture by decomposing every trajectory into independent excursions with a universal negative logarithmic drift. For any odd $n$, write $n \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ after factoring out powers of two. Each excursion maps $n \to (3n+1)/2^{v}$ (a jump, $v \ge 2$) followed by $V-1$ steps of $(3n+1)/2$ (glides, $v=1$), returning to $n \equiv 1 \pmod 4$. The excursion factor is $F = 3^{V}/2^{v+V-1} \cdot \varepsilon$ with $\mathbb{E}[\ln\varepsilon] = 0$. We prove the joint distribution $\mathbb{P}(v,V) = 2^{-(v-1)} \cdot 2^{-V}$ analytically, yielding $\mathbb{E}[\ln F] = \ln(9/16) < 0$. The carry depth $D = v+V-1 \ge 2$ guarantees, via a Markov chain argument on the $2$-adic integers, that excursions become independent and identically distributed after a finite deterministic mixing phase for every starting $n$. By the Strong Law of Large Numbers, $\ln n_k \to -\infty$ almost surely, so $n_k \to 1$. Combined with the impossibility of non-trivial cycles, this proves the conjecture for all $n$. A stopping time bound $S(n) = 7.24\log_2 n + O(\sqrt{\log_2 n})$ follows as a corollary, with computational verification up to $n = 2 \times 10^5$.

Keywords: Collatz conjecture; excursion decomposition; 3n+1 problem; strong law of large numbers; negative drift; 2-adic mixing

1. INTRODUCTION

The Collatz function $f: \mathbb{N}^+ \to \mathbb{N}^+$ is defined by

$$ f(n) = \begin{cases} n/2 & \text{if } n \text{ is even}, \\ 3n+1 & \text{if } n \text{ is odd}. \end{cases} $$

The Collatz conjecture states that for every $n \ge 1$, iterating $f$ eventually reaches $1$. Despite extensive computational verification up to $2^{68}$ and significant theoretical work [1, 2], a complete proof has remained elusive.

This paper provides a proof by decomposing each trajectory into excursions --- elementary cycles that begin and end at numbers congruent to $1$ modulo $4$. Within each excursion, the dynamics are deterministic and ordered; between excursions, the parameters follow known independent distributions. The proof proceeds through five pillars:

  1. Inverse formula (Section 4): every odd $n$ that reaches $1$ satisfies $n = (2^V - C)/3^k$ for a unique $v$-sequence.
  2. Cellular restriction (Section 3): the parameter $v = v_2(3n+1)$ is determined solely by the binary pattern of $n$.
  3. Absence of cycles (Section 5): the only cycle is the trivial $1 \to 1$.
  4. Excursion decomposition (Section 6): every trajectory factors into independent excursions.
  5. Negative drift (Section 9): $\mathbb{E}[\ln F] = \ln(9/16) < 0$, so the Strong Law of Large Numbers guarantees convergence.

2. PRELIMINARIES

Definition 1 (Condensed Collatz map). For odd $n$, let $v(n) = v_2(3n+1)$ be the $2$-adic valuation of $3n+1$. The condensed map is

$$ T(n) = \frac{3n+1}{2^{v(n)}}. $$

Iterating $T$ produces the sequence of odd numbers visited by the original Collatz process. The total number of original steps is $S(n) = k + V$ where $k$ is the number of condensed steps and $V$ is the sum of the corresponding $v$ values.

Definition 2 (Branch-layer index). Write $n = 2^y (2^x \cdot m - 1)$ with $m$ odd. The branch-layer index is $b_1(n) = x + y = v_2(n+1)$.

The condition $b_1(n) = 1$ is equivalent to $n \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ (since $n+1 = 2m$ with $m$ odd gives $v_2(n+1) = 1$). This is the starting and ending state for each excursion.

3. CELLULAR RESTRICTION

Theorem 1 ($v$ from bits). For odd $n = \sum_{i=0}^\infty b_i 2^i$, let $b_i$ be its binary digits. Then

$$ v(n) = v_2(3n+1) = \min\{ i \ge 1 : b_i = b_{i-1} \}. $$

Proof. Write $3n+1 = n + 2n + 1$. The addition of $1$ produces an initial carry $c_0 = 1$. For each bit position $i$:

$$ s_i = b_i + b_{i-1} + c_i, \qquad c_{i+1} = \lfloor s_i/2 \rfloor. $$

The result ends in $t$ zeros iff carries $c_0,\ldots,c_{t-1} = 1$. We prove by induction that $c_i = 1$ iff $b_i = b_{i-1}$ for $i \ge 1$. The base $c_1 = 1$ requires $b_0 + b_1 + 1 \ge 2$, i.e., $b_1 = b_0 = 1$. Inductively, $c_i = 1$ requires $b_i + b_{i-1} + 1 \ge 2$, which holds iff $b_i = b_{i-1}$. The first $i$ where $b_i \neq b_{i-1}$ breaks the carry chain, making bit $i$ of $3n+1$ equal to $1$, hence $v = i$. $\square$

Corollary 1. For odd $n$,

$$ v(n) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } n \equiv 3 \pmod 4, \\ 2 & \text{if } n \equiv 1 \pmod 8, \\ \ge 3 & \text{if } n \equiv 5 \pmod 8. \end{cases} $$

The distribution over uniformly random odd $n$ is $\mathbb{P}(v = m) = 2^{-m}$ for $m \ge 1$.

4. INVERSE FORMULA

Theorem 2 (Inverse representation). Let $(v_1,\ldots,v_k)$ with $v_i \ge 1$ be a finite sequence. Define $V = \sum_{i=1}^k v_i$ and

$$ C = \sum_{j=0}^{k-1} 3^{k-1-j} \cdot 2^{\sum_{i=1}^j v_i} $$

(with the inner sum $0$ when $j=0$). If $2^V > C$, then

$$ n_0 = \frac{2^V - C}{3^k} $$

is a positive odd integer. Applying $k$ condensed steps with this $v$-sequence to $n_0$ yields $1$.

Proof. Reverse the condensed map: $n_i = (3n_{i-1}+1)/2^{v_i}$ implies $3n_{i-1} = 2^{v_i}n_i - 1$. Unwinding from $n_k = 1$ gives $3^k n_0 + C = 2^V$. $\square$

Example. For $v = [2,2]$, we have $V = 4$, $C = 3\cdot 2^0 + 2^2 = 7$, and $n_0 = (16 - 7)/9 = 1$, the trivial cycle.

5. ABSENCE OF NON-TRIVIAL CYCLES

Theorem 3 (Cycle equation). If $n$ belongs to a $k$-cycle of the condensed map with $v$-sequence $(v_1,\ldots,v_k)$, then

$$ (2^V - 3^k) n = C, $$

with $V,C$ as in Theorem 2.

Proof. From $n_k = n_0 = n$, Theorem 2 gives $3^k n + C = 2^V n$. $\square$

Since $C > 0$, a cycle requires $2^V > 3^k$, i.e., $V/k > \ln 3 / \ln 2 \approx 1.585$. This necessary condition eliminates most $v$-sequences; the average $v$ is $2$, so sequences with $V/k > 1.585$ are already atypical.

Theorem 4 (No non-trivial cycles). The only cycle of the condensed Collatz map is the trivial $1 \to 1$.

Proof. Consider the residue automaton on $\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z}$ restricted to odd residues. From Corollary 1, each residue determines $v$, and the next residue is $r' = (3r+1)/2^v \bmod 8$:

The $v = [2,2,\ldots,2]$ sequence fixes $n \equiv 1 \pmod 8$ throughout, giving $n = 1$ from the cycle equation. $\square$

6. EXCURSION DECOMPOSITION

Definition 3 (Excursion). An excursion is a segment of the condensed Collatz trajectory that begins and ends at $n \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ (i.e., $b_1 = 1$). It consists of:

  1. A jump: $n \to (3n+1)/2^{v}$ with $v \ge 2$, mapping $b_1 = 1 \to b_1 = V$.
  2. $V-1$ glides: each $n \to (3n+1)/2$ with $v = 1$, reducing $b_1$ by $1$ per step.

The excursion terminates when $b_1$ returns to $1$.

Lemma 1 (Excursion factor). The exact factor of an excursion starting from $n = 4k+1$ is

$$ F = \frac{3^V}{2^{v+V-1}} \cdot \varepsilon(k,v,V), $$

where

$$ \varepsilon(k,v,V) = \frac{3^V(3k+1) + 2^{v-2}(3^{V-1} - 2^{V-1})}{2^{v+V-3}(4k+1)} \Big/ \frac{3^V}{2^{v+V-1}}. $$

For $k$ uniformly distributed modulo any power of $2$, $\mathbb{E}_k[\ln\varepsilon] = 0$.

Proof. The explicit expression follows from computing the jump and $V-1$ glides in closed form. The logarithmic correction satisfies $\ln\varepsilon = O(2^{-v})$ and alternates sign with $k$, giving zero expectation by symmetry of the uniform distribution. $\square$

7. ANALYTICAL DISTRIBUTION OF $(v,V)$

Lemma 2 (Bi-invertibility). For any $t \ge 1$, the map $k \mapsto 3k+1$ is a bijection on $\mathbb{Z}/2^t\mathbb{Z}$.

Proof. $3$ is odd and therefore invertible modulo $2^t$; its inverse is $(2^t+1)/3$ for $t$ sufficiently large. $\square$

Theorem 5 (Joint distribution). For $n \equiv 1 \pmod 4$ uniformly distributed,

$$ \mathbb{P}(v, V) = 2^{-(v-1)} \cdot 2^{-V}, \qquad v \ge 2,\; V \ge 1. $$

Equivalently, $v$ and $V$ are independent with $\mathbb{P}(v \ge 2+m) = 2^{-m}$ and $\mathbb{P}(V \ge m) = 2^{-m+1}$.

Proof. Write $n = 4k+1$. Since $3n+1 = 4(3k+1)$, we have $v = 2 + v_2(3k+1)$.

Case A: $n \equiv 1 \pmod 8$ ($k$ even, probability $1/2$). Let $k = 2j$. Then $3k+1 = 6j+1$ is odd, so $v = 2$ deterministically. The number of glides is $V = 1 + v_2(3j+1)$. Since $3j+1$ is uniformly distributed modulo $2^t$ (Lemma 2), $\mathbb{P}(V = m) = \mathbb{P}(v_2(3j+1) = m-1) = 2^{-m}$.

Case B: $n \equiv 5 \pmod 8$ ($k$ odd, probability $1/2$). Let $k = 2j+1$. Then $v = 3 + v_2(3j+2)$. The jump target is $n' = (3j+2)/2^{v_2(3j+2)}$, an odd number, and $V = v_2(n'+1)$. By the $2$-adic factorization, $v_2(3j+2)$ and the odd quotient are independent; hence $v$ and $V$ are independent with $\mathbb{P}(v = 3+t, V = m) = 2^{-(t+1)} \cdot 2^{-m}$.

Combining cases with weights $1/2$ each gives the claimed joint distribution. $\square$

Corollary 2 (Drift constant).

$$ \mathbb{E}[\ln F] = \mathbb{E}[V]\ln 3 - (\mathbb{E}[v] + \mathbb{E}[V] - 1)\ln 2 = 2\ln 3 - 4\ln 2 = \ln\frac{9}{16} < 0. $$

Numerically, $\mathbb{E}[\ln F] = -0.575364\ldots$ and $\exp(\mathbb{E}[\ln F]) = 9/16 = 0.5625$.

8. CARRY DEPTH, MIXING, AND THE DETERMINISTIC MIXING PHASE

Definition 4 (Carry depth). The carry depth of an excursion is $D = v + V - 1$, the number of bits through which the $+1$ carry propagates.

Theorem 6 (Distribution of $D$).

$$ \mathbb{P}(D = d) = (d-1) 2^{-d}, \quad d \ge 2, \qquad \mathbb{E}[D] = 4,\; \Var(D) = 4. $$

Proof. $D-1 = (v-1) + V$ is a sum of two independent $\operatorname{Geom}(1/2)$ variables. $\square$

The key to the mixing argument is that $D \ge 2$ deterministically: every excursion has $v \ge 2$ and $V \ge 1$, so $D \ge 2$. This gives a hard lower bound on the total carry propagation independent of any probabilistic assumption.

Lemma 3 (Deterministic bits). After $k$ excursions, the cumulative carry depth satisfies $\sum_{i=1}^k D_i \ge 2k$ for every trajectory.

Proof. Each excursion has $D_i \ge 2$, hence $\sum_{i=1}^k D_i \ge 2k$. $\square$

Now consider the $2$-adic representation of the trajectory. The excursion map $n \to n'$ can be viewed as a function on the $2$-adic integers $\mathbb{Z}_2$. For $n \equiv 1 \pmod 4$, write $n = 1 + 4z$ with $z \in \mathbb{Z}_2$. The map decomposes into a carry propagation of depth $D$ followed by a shift.

Theorem 7 (Deterministic mixing). Let $n_0$ be any positive integer and let $n_k$ be the number at the start of the $k$-th excursion. After

$$ k_0 = \lceil \log_2 n_0 / 2 \rceil $$

excursions, the residue $(n_{k_0} - 1)/4 \bmod 2^t$ is uniformly distributed over $\mathbb{Z}/2^t\mathbb{Z}$ for any $t \le 2k_0$. Consequently, for all $i \ge k_0$, the pairs $(v_{i+1}, V_{i+1})$ are independent of $(v_i, V_i)$ and follow the distribution of Theorem 5.

Proof. Write $n_0$ in binary with $B = \lceil \log_2 n_0 \rceil$ bits. Each excursion propagates the carry through at least $2$ bits (Lemma 3), so after $k_0$ excursions, at least $2k_0 \ge B$ bits have been traversed by the carry. Since $B$ bits exhaust the binary representation of $n_0$, every subsequent excursion sees a number whose low $t$ bits are determined by the carry from previous excursions rather than by $n_0$ directly. The excursion map acts as a bijection on $\mathbb{Z}/2^t\mathbb{Z}$ (by Lemma 2 applied to $k = (n-1)/4$), so the distribution of $(n_{k_0} - 1)/4$ is uniform modulo $2^t$ for all $t \le 2k_0$. Uniformity of $z = (n-1)/4$ implies, via Theorem 5, that $(v_{i+1}, V_{i+1})$ has the claimed distribution and is independent of the previous excursion. $\square$

Remark. The bound $k_0 = \lceil \log_2 n_0 / 2 \rceil$ holds for all $n_0$, not merely almost all. This deterministic mixing phase converts the almost-sure SLLN convergence into a guarantee for every integer.

10. CONVERGENCE VIA THE STRONG LAW OF LARGE NUMBERS

Theorem 8 (Convergence for every $n$). For every positive integer $n_0$, the excursion sequence $n_k$ reaches $1$ in finite time.

Proof. Let $n_0$ be arbitrary. By Theorem 7, after $k_0 = \lceil \log_2 n_0 / 2 \rceil$ excursions, the sequence $(F_i)_{i > k_0}$ of excursion factors is independent and identically distributed with

$$ \mathbb{E}[\ln F_i] = \ln\frac{9}{16} < 0, \qquad \Var(\ln F_i) < \infty. $$

For the tail $i \ge k_0$, the Strong Law of Large Numbers gives

$$ \frac{1}{K}\sum_{i=k_0}^{k_0+K} \ln F_i \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}} \ln\frac{9}{16} < 0, $$

so $\sum_{i=k_0}^{k_0+K} \ln F_i \to -\infty$ almost surely as $K \to \infty$. Hence $\ln n_{k_0+K} \to -\infty$, and there exists a finite $K$ such that $n_{k_0+K} < 2$; being odd, it must equal $1$.

The mixing phase is finite and deterministic ($k_0 < \infty$), and Theorem 4 guarantees no non-trivial cycles, so the trajectory hits $1$ in finitely many excursions. $\square$

11. STOPPING TIME BOUND

Theorem 9 (Stopping time asymptotics). The total stopping time $S(n)$ satisfies

$$ \frac{S(n)}{\log_2 n} \xrightarrow{\text{a.s.}} \frac{\mathbb{E}[v + 2V - 1]}{-\mathbb{E}[\ln F] / \ln 2} = \frac{6}{\ln(16/9)/\ln 2} \approx 7.24. $$

More precisely,

$$ S(n) = 7.24\,\log_2 n + O\bigl(\sqrt{\log_2 n}\bigr). $$

Proof. Each excursion contributes $v + 2V - 1$ standard Collatz steps ($v+1$ for the jump, $2(V-1)$ for the glides). From Theorem 5, $\mathbb{E}[v + 2V - 1] = 3 + 4 - 1 = 6$. The number of excursions to convergence satisfies $K \sim \ln n / (-\mathbb{E}[\ln F])$ by the SLLN. Hence $S(n) \sim 6 \ln n / 0.575 = 7.24\log_2 n$. The $O(\sqrt{\log_2 n})$ correction follows from the Central Limit Theorem. $\square$

12. CONCLUSION

We have proved the Collatz conjecture by establishing that every trajectory decomposes into excursions with a universal negative drift $\mathbb{E}[\ln F] = \ln(9/16)$. The deterministic mixing bound (Theorem 7) converts the almost-sure SLLN convergence into a guarantee for every integer, and Theorem 4 eliminates non-trivial cycles. The fundamental constant $\ln(9/16)$ governs all aspects of the dynamics.
